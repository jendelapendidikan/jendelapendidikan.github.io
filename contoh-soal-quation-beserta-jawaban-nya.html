8. 1.Explain about various theories regarding the origin of state?Answer:2.From quation number one, how about u, Which one is held to be the valid theoryAnswer:3.Could you tell about Discuss the Social Contract Theory of Hobbes. What are its limitations?Answer:​​


Jawaban:

Various theories regarding the origin of the state:

There are several theories proposed by political philosophers and thinkers to explain the origin of the state. Some of the prominent theories include:

Divine Right Theory: This theory suggests that states were created by divine will or intervention, often with religious justification. It implies that rulers have a divine mandate to govern.

Evolutionary Theory: According to this theory, states gradually evolved from small, primitive communities. It's a gradual process of societal development.

Social Contract Theory: This theory, proposed by philosophers like Thomas Hobbes, John Locke, and Jean-Jacques Rousseau, posits that individuals came together and agreed to form a state to escape the state of nature and ensure their security and well-being.

Force Theory: This theory suggests that states originated through the use of force. Powerful individuals or groups established control over a territory, and this control eventually became a state.

Patriarchal Theory: This theory argues that the state evolved from the natural authority of the father, who was the head of the family

This authority extended to larger social groups over time.

Marxist Theory: According to Karl Marx, states emerged as a tool of the ruling class to maintain their power and control over the working class.

Among these theories, the validity of a particular theory often depends on one's perspective and the historical and cultural context. There is no single universally accepted theory of the origin of the state. The choice of a valid theory may vary depending on one's political, philosophical, or historical viewpoint.

The Social Contract Theory of Hobbes:

Thomas Hobbes's Social Contract Theory argues that in the state of nature, life is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short." To escape this state of perpetual conflict and insecurity, individuals voluntarily agree to form a social contract, surrendering some of their natural rights to a central authority (the Leviathan) in exchange for protection and order. The limitations of Hobbes's theory include:

Authoritarianism: Hobbes's theory justifies strong central authority, which can lead to authoritarian rule, limiting individual freedoms.

Pessimistic View: It assumes humans are inherently selfish and in constant conflict, which might not reflect the reality of human nature.

Lack of Revolt: Hobbes's theory doesn't provide a basis for rebellion against a tyrannical government. It prioritizes stability over individual liberty.

Absence of Consent: Critics argue that individuals in the state of nature might never have consented to such a contract.

Limited Role of Law: Hobbes's theory doesn't emphasize the role of law, justice, or the rights of citizens within the state.

These limitations have led to various modifications and alternative theories in political philosophy.
